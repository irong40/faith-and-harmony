# SOP: Insurance Claim Documentation Shoot

**Mission Type:** Insurance Damage Documentation
**Typical Client:** Insurance adjusters, property owners, public adjusters, attorneys
**Frequency:** One-time (post-event)
**Estimated Flight Time:** 15-25 minutes
**Deliverable Turnaround:** 24 hours (rush: same day available)

---

## Pre-Shoot Checklist

- [ ] Confirm property address and client contact (adjuster or property owner)
- [ ] Get claim number and type of damage (storm, wind, hail, fire, flood, tree fall)
- [ ] Ask: is this for initial claim, supplemental, or dispute/litigation?
- [ ] Determine if the client has specific documentation requirements or forms
- [ ] Check weather — safe to fly, and similar conditions to damage event can add context
- [ ] Charge batteries (2-3)
- [ ] Review property on Google Maps — roof type, lot layout, access points
- [ ] Bring measurement reference (tape measure, known-size object for scale)
- [ ] Confirm no active hazards (downed power lines, structural instability)

## Equipment

- **Aircraft:** DJI Mini 4 Pro (residential) or Matrice 4E (commercial / large structures)
- **Batteries:** 2-3
- **Memory:** Formatted microSD, 64 GB+
- **Camera settings:** Highest resolution, RAW+JPG preferred (RAW for evidence integrity)
- **Thermal camera:** If available — moisture detection behind walls/under roofing
- **Ground gear:** Tape measure, scale reference card, clipboard with claim info
- **EXIF metadata:** Ensure GPS tagging is ON — critical for evidence

## Critical: Evidence Integrity

Insurance documentation may be used in legal proceedings. Follow these rules:

1. **Never alter, edit, or enhance photos** beyond basic brightness/contrast
2. **Never use sky replacement, object removal, or AI enhancement**
3. **Keep RAW files** — they serve as tamper-proof originals
4. **EXIF data must be intact** — GPS coordinates, timestamp, camera info
5. **Shoot in chronological order** — timestamps tell the story
6. **Narrate or log** each shot as you take it
7. **Chain of custody** — know who receives the files and document the handoff

## Site Arrival Protocol

1. Meet adjuster or property owner
2. Get verbal walkthrough of damage — what happened, when, what's been touched/repaired
3. Document the scene BEFORE anything is moved or cleaned up
4. Note and photograph any temporary repairs (tarps, boards) — these are part of the record
5. Identify safe launch zone away from damage debris
6. Check for downed lines, unstable structures, or other hazards

## Shot List

### Aerial — Mandatory

| # | Shot | Altitude | Camera Angle | Notes |
|---|------|----------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | Property context (4 directions) | 100-150 ft AGL | 30° | Shows property in neighborhood, establishes location |
| 2 | Full roof nadir | 60-80 ft AGL | 90° | Complete roof coverage |
| 3 | Each roof face (oblique) | 40-60 ft AGL | 45° | North, East, South, West |
| 4 | Damage overview | 40-80 ft AGL | 45° | Wide shot showing extent of damage area |
| 5 | Damage detail (close) | 15-30 ft AGL | 45-90° | Individual damage points — MULTIPLE angles each |
| 6 | Adjacent undamaged areas | 40-60 ft AGL | 45° | Shows contrast between damaged and intact |
| 7 | Surrounding properties | 100-150 ft AGL | 30° | Shows event affected the area (storm path, etc.) |

### Damage-Specific Shots

| Damage Type | Specific Shots Needed |
|------------|----------------------|
| **Wind** | Lifted/missing shingles, exposed underlayment, shifted ridge caps, debris field |
| **Hail** | Impact marks on shingles, gutters, HVAC units, vent caps (close-up critical) |
| **Tree fall** | Tree position, impact point, structural damage, root ball/break point |
| **Fire** | Char patterns, structural collapse, heat damage extent, adjacent exposure |
| **Flood** | Water line marks, debris deposits, erosion, foundation exposure |
| **General** | Flashing damage, gutter damage, siding damage, window damage, fence damage |

### Ground-Level — Mandatory

| # | Shot | Notes |
|---|------|-------|
| 8 | Property front with address visible | Establishes identity — include house number or mailbox |
| 9 | All four sides of structure | Ground-level documentation of each elevation |
| 10 | Damage from ground | Correlates with aerial damage shots |
| 11 | Debris/fallen materials | Where they landed, what they came from |
| 12 | Scale reference | Tape measure or reference card next to damage for size context |
| 13 | Interior damage (if applicable) | Water stains, ceiling damage — correlate with roof damage above |
| 14 | Temporary repairs | Tarps, boards, plastic — document what's been done |
| 15 | Gutters and downspouts | Dents, separation, overflow evidence |

## Flight Execution

1. Start wide — context shots from 100-150 ft establishing the property
2. Work inward — full roof coverage at 60-80 ft
3. Each face oblique at 40-60 ft
4. Damage areas: fly to 15-30 ft and photograph from **minimum 3 angles** per damage point
5. Photograph undamaged areas of same material for comparison
6. If hail: get very close to gutters, HVAC, and soft metals (show impact pattern)
7. Photograph surrounding properties to establish event scope
8. Land, ground-level walkthrough with scale references

**Key principle:** MORE shots is ALWAYS better for insurance. You can't go back if you missed something.

## Report Structure

1. **Cover page** — Property address, claim #, date of inspection, date of loss, inspector info
2. **Property overview** — Context photos, property description
3. **Damage summary** — Bullet points of all findings
4. **Detailed findings** — Each damage area with numbered photos and descriptions
5. **Comparison evidence** — Damaged vs. undamaged, before/after if available
6. **Scope of damage** — Area/neighborhood context showing event impact
7. **Recommendations** — Repair vs. replace assessment (if qualified to provide)
8. **Photo appendix** — All remaining photos with captions

## Legal / Compliance Notes

- **You are documenting, not adjusting.** Do not make coverage determinations unless licensed.
- Keep all original files unmodified — they may be subpoenaed
- If asked to testify: your photos and flight logs are your evidence
- Carry E&O (Errors and Omissions) insurance for professional liability
- Document your methodology — if challenged, you can explain your process
- EXIF data + flight logs from Trestle = strong evidence chain

## Safety Notes

- Post-storm sites may have hidden hazards: downed lines, gas leaks, structural weakness
- Never land on or near a damaged roof
- If structure appears unstable, do NOT enter — aerial documentation only
- Watch for temporary tarps and loose materials that can blow into props
- Debris fields may contain nails, glass, and sharp materials — wear boots
